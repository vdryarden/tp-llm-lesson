## lon as a main verb

The word "lon" can serve as the main predicate of a sentence, meaning "to exist",
"to be present", or "to be alive":

> mi wile lon. -- I want to exist / I want to live.

> ijo li lon ala. -- It doesn't exist. / It's not real. / It's not happening.

> sina lon ala la mi wile ala lon. -- If you're not here, I don't want to be alive.
> (Without you, I have no reason to live.)

Combined with "la": English "what you are [verb]-ing" becomes "sina [verb] e ijo la"
(not a noun phrase). The whole clause then acts as context:

> sina lukin e ijo la ona li lon ala. -- What you are seeing is not real.

> sina lukin e ijo la ona li lon ala. sina lukin e lipu la ona li lon ala.
> -- What you see and what you read is not happening.

## Time of day

To express parts of the day beyond "suno" (day) and "pimeja" (night):

> tenpo pi open suno -- morning ("time of sun-opening")

> tenpo pi suno sewi -- midday / noon ("time of sun above")

> tenpo pi suno anpa -- afternoon ("time of descending sun")

Examples:
> tenpo pi open suno la mi moku e pan. -- In the morning I eat bread.

> tenpo pi suno anpa la ona li lape. -- In the afternoon she sleeps.

## Near and far time

"tenpo poka" (nearby time) means "soon" or "in the short term".
"tenpo weka" (far time) means "long from now" or "in the long term":

> tenpo poka la mi tawa. -- I will go soon.

> tenpo weka la ijo li ante. -- In the long run, things change.

> sina kama e pilin pona lon tenpo poka la ni li kama e pilin ike lon tenpo weka.
> -- Forcing happiness in the short term makes you feel worse in the long term.

## kama e X (causing something)

When "kama" takes a direct object with "e", it means "to cause" or "to bring about":

> sina kama e ni. -- You caused this.

> ale li kama e pilin ike. -- Everything causes bad feelings / Everything is depressing.

> ale li anpa e mi mute la mi mute li tawa musi lon anpa.
> -- When life forces us to our knees, we dance the limbo.

Note: "anpa e X" means "to push/force X down" or "to defeat X".

## pali sama ni: (pretending)

The phrase "pali sama ni:" (act like this:) is used to describe pretending or
acting as if something were the case:

> jan Ton li pali sama ni: ona li kute ala. -- Tom pretended not to have heard anything.

> ona li pali sama ni: ona li sona e ni. -- She pretended to know this.

## wan taso as topic

"[subject] wan taso la" sets a context meaning "when [subject] is alone":

> mi wan taso la tawa li pona tawa mi. -- I like to travel alone.

> sina wan taso la o awen. -- Stay safe when you're alone.

## linja lawa (hair)

"linja lawa" (head-strings) is the standard compound for hair:

> linja lawa mi li suli. -- My hair is long.

> linja lawa ona li jelo. -- Their hair is yellow/blonde.

## sinpin lawa (face)

"sinpin lawa" (front of the head) means "face":

> ona li jo e sinpin lawa pona. -- They have a good face / They are good-looking.

> jan pi kulupu mama li jo e sinpin lawa sama. -- Family members have similar faces.

## pilin ala e X (no confidence / no trust)

"pilin ala e X" means "to have no feeling toward X" — used to express lack of
confidence, trust, or attachment:

> mi pilin ala e jan ni. -- I have no confidence in this person. / I don't trust this person.

> tenpo ala la mi pilin ala e ijo. -- I never had confidence in this matter.
