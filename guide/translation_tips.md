% toki pona - translation strategies and idioms
% translation guide
% 2026-06-18

This page collects practical patterns for translating English into natural toki pona.
The goal is not word-for-word mapping but finding the toki pona expression that
communicates the same meaning.

---

## Prefer restructured sentences over literal translation

Toki pona often expresses an idea with a completely different sentence structure
than English. When a literal translation feels awkward, try restructuring.

Instead of:

> "This made me feel close to him."
> ~~ni li pana e pilin sama tawa mi tawa ona.~~

Restructure using `tan ni la` ("because of this") and `toki e ni`:

> tan ni la mi pilin e ni: mi lon poka ona.
> — Because of this, I feel (that) I am near him.

Instead of:

> "The science makes me sleepy."
> ~~sona ni li pana e lape tawa mi.~~

Restructure so the result is the main clause:

> mi kama lape tan nasin sona ni.
> — I fall asleep because of this science.

General rule: lead with the person who experiences something, not the abstract cause.

---

## kama + verb = "become" or "come to"

`kama` before a verb expresses becoming, starting to, or a change of state.

| English              | toki pona             |
|----------------------|-----------------------|
| fall asleep          | kama lape             |
| get food / receive   | kama jo               |
| become happy         | kama pilin pona       |
| learn (= come to know)| kama sona            |
| arrive               | kama lon              |

Examples:
> mi kama jo e suwi lete lon esun. — I got a cold sweet thing at the store.
> ona li kama lape. — He fell asleep.

## Expressing "X likes Y" — use tawa

The idiomatic way to say someone likes something is:

> Y li pona tawa X. — X likes Y. (lit. "Y is good from X's perspective")

> pan li pona mute tawa ona. — He really likes bread. ("He's a bread junkie.")
> moku seme li pona tawa sina? — What food do you like?

Avoid the literal `ona li olin e pan` ("he loves bread") unless you mean emotional attachment.

---

## Reflexive pronouns — ona sama

To express "himself", "herself", "themselves", use `ona sama` (lit. "same-self of them").

> ona li lukin e ona sama lon jasima. — He is looking at himself in the mirror.
> mi pona e mi sama. — I improve myself.

Without `ona sama`, the sentence means looking at a mirror as an object, not reflexively.

---

## Gender disambiguation — ona meli / ona mije

`ona` is gender-neutral. When English specifies "she" or "he" and the distinction
matters (especially when multiple people appear in the same sentence), use:

- `ona meli` — she
- `ona mije` — he

> ona meli li toki e wile sona ni: nasin seme la ona meli li kama lon tomo?
> — She asked (another woman) how to get to the building.

Use `ona` alone when gender is irrelevant or clear from context.

---

## Noun specificity — stack adjectives to narrow meaning

When a basic noun is too broad, add adjectives to narrow it:

| English    | basic toki pona | more specific          |
|------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| apple      | kili loje       | kili sike loje (round red fruit) |
| ice cream  | moku lete       | suwi lete (cold sweet thing)     |
| elevator   | ilo tawa        | ilo pi tawa sewi (upward-moving tool) |
| train station | tomo tawa   | tomo pi tomo tawa linja (house of rail-vehicle) |

The more specific version is usually more natural and precise.

---

## "Get/receive" — kama jo, not moku

For "buy", "receive", "get", use `kama jo` (lit. "come to have"):

> ona li kama jo e suwi lete lon esun. — They bought a cold treat at the store.
> sina ken pana e lipu lili tawa mi mute? — Could you send us a brochure?

`kama jo` with `lon esun` (at the store) covers buying. `kama jo` without location covers receiving.

---

## "Also" at the end of a predicate — kin

The word `kin` (or its synonym `a`) can be added at the end of a clause to mean
"also", "too", "as well":

> ona li jan pi pona sijelo, li jan pi sitelen toki kin.
> — He is a doctor and also a novelist.

Without `kin`, the second predicate reads as just an additional fact, not one
marked as "also".

---

## Expressing courage/willingness — wile vs pilin wawa

"Dare to" or "have the courage to" is not the same as "want to" (`wile`).
Use `pilin wawa` (feel strong/confident) or express it as an emotional state:

> ona li pilin wawa ala ni: ona li ken toki e ni tawa mama ona.
> — He does not feel confident enough to tell this to his parents.

## Proper names — quick phonetic reference

When adapting English names to toki pona unofficial words:

- Final consonants: drop them or add a vowel. "Tom" → `Ton` (not `Tama`)
- `r` and `l` both map to `l`. "Robert" → `Lope`
- `b`, `f`, `v` → `p`. "David" → `Tawi`
- `g` → `k`. "George" → `Sosi` (via English pronunciation /dʒɔrdʒ/)
- `sh`, `ch`, `j` → `s`. "John" → `San`
- No double vowels. "Lee" → `Li`
- Forbidden sequences (ji, ti, wo, wu) are replaced: "Tim" → `Tin`

Always base the adaptation on how the name is *pronounced*, not how it is spelled.
Use `jan [Name]` for people: `jan Ton` (Tom), `jan San` (John), `jan Mewi` (Mary).

---

## Causative sentences — pana e pilin vs restructuring

For "X caused Y to feel Z", you can use `pana e pilin` but the restructured form
with `la` is usually more natural:

> tan ni la mi pilin pona. — Because of this, I feel good. (idiomatic)
> ni li pana e pilin pona tawa mi. — This gave good feelings to me. (more literal)

Both are correct; the `la`-restructured form is preferred in fluent toki pona.

---

## Standing / posture verbs

Body postures don't have dedicated verbs; use location words:

| English       | toki pona                   |
|---------------|-----------------------------|
| stand         | lon sinpin (be at the front/upright) |
| sit           | lon anpa (be low/down)      |
| lie down      | lon lape (be in rest position) |

> mi mute li lon sinpin. — We stand. (lit. "We are upright/forward")

---

## Checking comprehension vs correctness

When asking "Did you check/review this?", note the difference:

- `sina lukin e sitelen ni anu seme?` — Did you look at this text? (physically read it)
- `sina lukin e pona pi sitelen ni anu seme?` — Did you check the quality of this text? (review it)

The second asks about the quality (`pona`) of the thing, which is the intent of "check".

---

## Misunderstanding — sona ike vs kute ike

"Misunderstand" can be expressed two ways depending on the source of the error:

- `sona ike e toki mi` — understood my words badly (wrong interpretation)
- `kute ike e toki mi` — heard my words badly (wrong perception/reception)

`kute ike` (hear badly) is often more idiomatic for general misunderstandings in conversation.
